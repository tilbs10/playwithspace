# Blog Series Outlines — "The World Cup Is a Spacing Masterclass"

**Series premise:** The World Cup is on, and it's the best free basketball
lesson running. Soccer and basketball are both invasion sports — same
problem (create and use space against an organised defence), different
surface. Because soccer's pitch is bigger and goals are rarer, the spacing
concepts play out in slow motion. You can *see* them. This series takes one
soccer concept per post and translates it into the basketball fundamentals
we work on every session.

**Series thread:** Every post ends the same way — the concept isn't about
the player with the ball. It's about the other players and where they are.
The final post pays that off: great players shine *because* the team's
spacing lets them.

**Status:** post 2 (patience) published · post 3 (timed runs) published ·
post 6 (closer, re-anchored on Spain's actual World Cup win + Rodri's
Golden Ball) published as "Great Players Don't Create Space Alone" ·

**Note on examples:** reference patterns, not specific match results — keeps

## Post 1 — Same Game, Bigger Pitch (series opener)

```yaml
title: "Same Game, Bigger Pitch: Why a Basketball Coach Is Watching the World Cup"
category: fundamentals
tags: [spacing, world-cup-series, cross-sport, basketball-iq]
type: article
```

### Why I'm watching soccer
- The World Cup is on and I can't stop seeing basketball in it.
- Invasion sports share one core problem: 10 (or 22) players, one ball,
  limited space. Whoever uses the space better wins.
- Soccer is spacing *in slow motion* — bigger field, fewer scores, so the
  build-up is visible in a way basketball's 24-second bursts aren't.

### The five concepts this series covers
- Patient build-up → not forcing the first available shot
- Well-timed runs → cutting and filling lanes at the right moment
- Passing angles → getting somewhere the ball can actually reach you
- Switching the attack → ball reversal and the weak side
- Spacing lets stars shine → why the best player needs the other four

### How to watch a match like a coach
- Watch one player *without* the ball for two minutes. Where do they go?
  When do they stand still?
- Watch the team that doesn't have the ball. What shape are they holding?
- The question I give my players: *"Who created that goal — the scorer, or
  someone who never touched the ball?"*

### What this teaches
- Basketball IQ isn't basketball-specific. It's spatial literacy.
- Frame for parents/coaches: don't tell kids to study film — watch a World
  Cup game together and ask one question at half-time.

### Homework (instead of a drill)
- Pick any match this week. Watch the first 10 minutes only tracking one
  midfielder. Bring one observation to training.

## Post 2 — Patience Is an Attacking Skill

```yaml
title: "Patience Is an Attacking Skill: What Build-Up Play Teaches Young Basketballers"
category: fundamentals
tags: [spacing, world-cup-series, offence, decision-making, patience]
type: article
```

### The thing that confuses new soccer watchers
- Why do they keep passing it backwards? Isn't the goal *that* way?
- Because the first forward option is usually the trap. The defence is set,
  compact, waiting for exactly that pass.
- Build-up play isn't slow because teams can't go faster. It's slow on
  purpose — every pass makes the defence move, and moving defences make
  mistakes.

### The youth basketball version of the problem
- The most common youth turnover: the very first pass, forced into traffic,
  because "forward = good".
- Kids equate fast with aggressive. But a rushed pass into a set defence
  isn't aggressive — it's a gift.
- Reframe: passing around the perimeter isn't "not attacking". It's the
  attack. You're moving defenders until one of them is wrong.

### What patience actually looks for
- Soccer: the line-breaking pass appears *after* the defence has been
  shifted side to side. You're not waiting for time to pass — you're
  waiting for a specific picture.
- Basketball: same thing. Swing, swing, and watch for a defender caught
  ball-watching, a late closeout, help leaning the wrong way.
- Key distinction to teach: patient ≠ passive. Every pass has a purpose —
  probe, shift, repeat.

### The drill worth trying
- **"Earn the entry"**: 5-out, no dribble, minimum 4 perimeter passes
  before any drive or entry pass — *but* the 4-pass count resets if a pass
  is lazy or a player receives it standing still.
- Progression: add token defence; attackers score double if the basket
  comes within two passes of a defensive mistake they can name afterwards.
- The question to ask mid-drill: *"What did that pass do to the defence?"*
  If the answer is "nothing", it was a wasted pass, not a patient one.

### What this teaches
- The goal isn't to make passes. It's to make the defence move until space
  appears — then take it immediately. Patience *and* ruthlessness.

## Post 3 — The Run Starts Before the Pass

```yaml
title: "The Run Starts Before the Pass: Timing Is a Skill You Can Teach"
category: fundamentals
tags: [spacing, world-cup-series, off-ball, cutting, transition]
type: article
```

### The most repeated moment in the World Cup
- A striker starts a run behind the defence, and the pass arrives into
  space that was empty when the ball left the passer's foot.
- Two players agreeing on a future: the passer plays the ball to where the
  runner *will be*. Neither can do it alone.
- The offside rule makes timing brutal in soccer — go too early and the
  play is dead. Basketball has no offside, so bad timing just quietly
  fails, and nobody learns from it.

### What "too early" and "too late" look like on a basketball court
- Too early: cutting before your defender's head turns — you arrive covered
  and clog the space (link back to spacing post: now you've *broken* the
  spacing).
- Too late: cutting after the ball-handler has picked up their dribble or
  looked away — the window existed and closed.
- The trigger concept: runs/cuts aren't scheduled, they're *triggered* —
  by a defender ball-watching, by the ball-handler's eyes, by a teammate
  clearing the space you're about to use.

### Transition is where this shows up most
- Soccer's counter-attack = basketball's fast break. Watch how runners fill
  lanes *wide* first, then attack — they don't chase the ball, they run to
  where the ball will want to go.
- Ties directly to our transition sessions: run the lane, not the ball.

### The drill worth trying
- **"Green light cuts"**: 3-on-3 half court, no screens. A cutter can only
  go when they see a trigger (defender turns head, ball-handler makes eye
  contact). After each possession, ask the cutter: *"What did you see that
  made you go?"* If the answer is "I just felt like it", replay it.
- Progression: passer gets a bonus point for hitting a cutter in stride —
  rewards the passer for seeing the run early.

### What this teaches
- Where to go is half the answer. *When* to go is the other half — and
  timing is readable, teachable, and rewatchable every night of the
  tournament.

## Post 4 — Get Off the Straight Line: Passing Angles

```yaml
title: "Get Off the Straight Line: What Soccer's Triangles Teach About Passing Angles"
category: fundamentals
tags: [spacing, world-cup-series, passing, support-play]
type: article
```

### The three steps nobody notices
- Watch a midfielder when a teammate wins the ball: they immediately take
  three small steps sideways. No sprint, no highlight. Those steps turn an
  impossible pass into an easy one.
- Soccer coaches call it a support angle. The best teams form triangles
  everywhere — because a triangle always gives the ball two options that
  aren't through a defender.

### The straight-line problem in youth basketball
- Kids stand directly behind their defender and call for the ball. The
  passing lane doesn't exist, and they experience it as "nobody passes to
  me".
- The fix isn't "get open" (too vague). It's geometric: if you can draw a
  straight line from the ball to you without touching a defender, you're
  open. If you can't, *move until you can*.
- Small moves beat big moves: an L-cut, two steps to the wing, lifting from
  the corner. Angles are created in metres, not sprints.

### The passer's half of the angle
- Angles are a two-player deal: the receiver moves to open the lane, the
  passer uses a fake or a dribble to shift the defender and *widen* it.
- Watch how rarely a World Cup pass goes to a standing target — the ball
  and the receiver arrive at the angle together.

### The drill worth trying
- **"Broken triangle"**: groups of 3 with one defender in the middle
  (rondo-style — steal soccer's own drill). The two off-ball players must
  keep a clean line to the ball at all times; if the defender shadows the
  lane, they *must* move to re-open it before they can receive.
- Coaching cue: *"Can the ball see you?"* — the fastest way to make lane
  awareness concrete for Yr 4–6.

### What this teaches
- "Get open" is a spacing instruction, not an effort instruction. Openness
  is a line on the floor, and players who learn to see the line stop
  needing to be told.

## Post 5 — The Ball Moves Faster Than the Defence: Switching the Attack

```yaml
title: "The Ball Moves Faster Than the Defence: Switching Sides to Create Opportunity"
category: fundamentals
tags: [spacing, world-cup-series, ball-movement, weak-side, offence]
type: article
```

### The most satisfying pass in soccer
- The 40-metre diagonal that switches play: one moment the attack looks
  dead, ten defenders crowded around the ball — then one pass and a winger
  is alone with space in front of them.
- Why it works: defences shift toward the ball as a unit. Every defender
  who slides toward the ball leaves space behind and beside them. The far
  side of the pitch is *always* the least defended.
- The overload is deliberate: good teams crowd one side *on purpose* to
  make the far side empty, then strike.

### Basketball's version: ball reversal and the skip pass
- Help defence = the whole defence tilting toward the ball, same as soccer.
- One swing pass rarely beats a defence. Side-to-side *reversal* does,
  because defenders must close out, recover, and close out again — and the
  third rotation is always slower than the first.
- The weak side corner is youth basketball's 40-metre diagonal: chronically
  empty, chronically ignored by kids fixated on the ball side.
- Connects to the gravity post: the overload IS gravity, applied by five
  players instead of one star.

### Why kids don't throw this pass
- They don't see the far side (ball-fixation, again), and the long pass
  feels risky. Reframe: against a shifted defence, the cross-court pass is
  the *safe* one — the risky pass is the one into the crowd.

### The drill worth trying
- **"Overload and switch"**: 4-on-4 half court. Offence must complete two
  passes on one side (pulling help across), then reverse the ball to the
  weak side within two passes. Score off a reversal = 3 points, any other
  basket = 1.
- The question mid-drill: *"Where was the space when we scored — and when
  did it appear?"* (Answer: it appeared while the ball was still on the
  other side. Space is created before it's used.)

### What this teaches
- Attacking one side of the floor is really about what it does to the
  *other* side. The defence can only be strong in one place — make them
  choose, then change the place.

## Post 6 — Great Players Don't Create Space Alone (series closer)

```yaml
title: "Great Players Don't Create Space Alone: What the Stars Owe Their Spacing"
category: fundamentals
tags: [spacing, world-cup-series, teamwork, mindset, gravity]
type: article
```

### The highlight lies to you
- The World Cup moment everyone replays: a great player picks the ball up,
  beats two defenders, scores. It looks like an individual act.
- Rewind ten seconds and watch everyone else: a teammate dragged a defender
  wide. Another held the far post, pinning the back line. A midfielder's
  run pulled the cover away. The star received the ball with room to work
  — and *that room was manufactured*.
- The genius is real. But genius operates in space, and the space was a
  team decision.

### The full-circle moment (ties the series together)
- Patient build-up shifted the defence (post 2). A timed run pulled a
  defender (post 3). Support angles kept the ball alive (post 4). The
  switch found the weak side (post 5). *Then* the great player did the
  great thing.
- Every concept in this series is present in the ten seconds before the
  highlight starts.

### The basketball translation — both directions
- For the supporting players: your spacing is a contribution that never
  shows up in the stats. Holding the corner IS an assist that arrives two
  passes early. (Reprise of "your position is contributing" from the
  spacing post, and the gravity post's off-ball rules.)
- For the best player on a youth team: the space your teammates give you is
  a gift — use it, don't just admire it. Attack the room while it exists.
- For coaches/parents: when you praise the scorer, ask the team *"who made
  that possible?"* Make the invisible work visible, out loud, every time.

### What this teaches
- The question that started the series — *who created that goal?* — has a
  real answer now: everyone. Spacing is the reason the great players get
  to look great.
- The season-philosophy tie-in: if these kids finish the season knowing
  "where to go", they'll spend the rest of their basketball lives making
  the players around them better — and getting better because of the
  players around them.

### Homework (closing the loop from post 1)
- Watch one more match. This time, when the highlight happens, rewind ten
  seconds and count the players who touched the space before the ball got
  there. Bring the number to training.
