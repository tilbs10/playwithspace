## When one player pulls the defence

Every team has someone the defence worries about most. Maybe it's your go-to guy — the one they've scouted and game-planned for. Maybe it's just who's got the hot hand tonight, drained three in a row, and now everyone in the gym knows it.

Think about what defences do when Steph Curry gets the ball off a screen. They go under? He drills it. They fight over? He's already past them. So they start sending a second body — and the moment they do, someone on Golden State is open. That's gravity.

Either way, that player has the whole defence tilting towards them. And that's one of the most valuable things that can happen in a game — if the rest of the team knows how to use it.

## What gravity actually does

When a defender cheats towards the threat, they're leaving someone open. When two bodies collapse on a drive because they're scared of the ball-handler, someone somewhere has no one on them.

Watch LeBron in the post. Before he's even made a move, his defender has help already leaning his way — and that leaves a corner shooter with a step of separation. LeBron hasn't scored yet but he's already done the work.

Gravity doesn't score by itself. It scores by pulling defenders out of position — and the team has to see that and act on it quickly.

## The thing most teams get wrong

Here's what usually happens: the go-to guy starts getting extra attention, so they stop attacking. They become a passer. They fade to the corner, defer, try to be unselfish.

That kills the gravity immediately.

The defence relaxes. The extra attention disappears. And suddenly the advantage is gone.

The primary scorer has to stay aggressive. Keep attacking. Keep being the thing the defence is afraid of. Even when they pass, even when they don't score, the threat has to stay real. The moment they opt out, they let the defence off the hook.

## What the rest of the team needs to do

While the gravity player stays in attack mode, everyone else has one job: stay ready and stay moving.

Not watching. Not standing still. Actually moving off the ball — filling corners, drifting to wings, setting screens that stretch the defence even further away from the threat.

When your hot player drives, don't watch. Get to the spot where a kick-out means an open shot, not a scramble. Be ready before the pass comes.

The whole team is working together, even when the ball is only touching one person.

## Using them as a decoy

Sometimes the play is to deliberately run actions through your primary scorer knowing the defence will overcommit — and read off that reaction.

This is basically the entire Draymond Green playbook when Steph is on the court. Steph uses the screen, the defence panics and sends two bodies, and Draymond slips to the rim completely untouched. The ball goes to Draymond — but Steph created it by being a threat.

A drive by your best player collapses two defenders, and a skip pass finds an open shooter. A screen set for the hot hand brings pressure, which leaves the screener's man scrambling.

The key is that the primary scorer has to commit fully. Half-hearted decoys don't work. Defences read intent. If your best player drifts through a screen lazily, the defender doesn't panic. If they come off it like they're about to attack the rim, the defence flinches — and that flinch is the opportunity.

## When it all connects

When the gravity player stays aggressive and the team is reading off them, you stop looking like five individuals and start looking like a system.

Defences have to make impossible choices. Commit to the threat and leave someone open. Help off the open player and let the threat keep attacking. There's no good answer for them.

That's when the game gets easy. Not because everyone is equally dangerous — but because one player's threat is doing the work of creating chances for the whole team.
