## The three steps nobody notices

Spain won the World Cup, and the story everyone told was about possession — they kept the ball better than anyone and made the rest of the world chase it. But keeping the ball isn't a thing you do with the ball. It's a thing the other ten players do without it.

Watch a Spain midfielder the instant a teammate wins possession. They don't sprint anywhere. They take three small steps sideways — just enough to get off the straight line, so there's now a clean path between the ball and their feet. No highlight, no replay. Those three steps are the reason Spain [attempted nearly 900 passes in the final](https://news.northeastern.edu/2026/07/20/spain-world-cup-2026/) and lost the ball almost never.

Soccer coaches have a name for this: a support angle. And the best teams build them everywhere, constantly, forming little triangles all over the pitch — because a triangle always gives the player on the ball two ways out that don't run through a defender.

That's the whole idea, and it translates straight onto a basketball floor.

## "Get open" is not an instruction

Here's what happens on a youth team. A player stands directly behind the defender guarding them, waves an arm, and calls for the ball. The pass never comes — the defender is in the way — and the kid comes back to the bench convinced their teammates won't pass to them.

They weren't open. They were *visible*, which isn't the same thing.

The trouble is that "get open" is a vague instruction, so it produces vague movement — a bit of wandering, an arm in the air. What a player needs instead is something geometric and concrete:

> If you can draw a straight line from the ball to you without it touching a defender, you're open. If you can't, move until you can.

Notice how small the move is. The receiver doesn't sprint to the other side of the floor. They take two steps to the top of the key — off the straight line — and a dead pass becomes a live one. Angles are made in metres, not sprints. An L-cut, a step up from the corner, a lift to the wing: that's the whole skill.

The cue I use is a question, and it's the fastest way I've found to make this concrete for a nine-year-old: **"Can the ball see you?"** Not can *you* see the ball — every kid can see the ball. Can the *ball* see *you*, in a straight line, with nobody in the way? If the answer is no, she already knows what to do about it: move until the answer is yes.

## The passer owns half the angle

Here's the part that makes it a team skill and not a receiver's chore: an angle is a two-player deal.

The receiver moves to open the lane. But the passer can widen that same lane without anyone moving into a better spot — a ball fake pulls the defender one way, a single dribble shifts the angle, a look-off freezes the help. Watch how rarely a Spain pass goes to a player standing still: the ball and the receiver arrive at the angle *together*, because both of them worked to create it.

On a youth team this means the kid with the ball can't just wait for someone to come open. He's responsible for the angle too — moving the defence with a fake, changing the geometry with a dribble. Two players solving one line.

## The drill worth trying

Steal soccer's own drill: the rondo. In basketball terms, it's the **broken triangle**.

Three attackers, one defender, in a tight space — say the top of the key and both wings. The attackers keep the ball; the defender in the middle tries to touch it. One rule that makes it a spacing drill instead of a keep-away game: the two players without the ball must always keep a clean line to it. The moment the defender slides into a passing lane, the two off-ball players have to *move to re-open an angle* before the ball can come.

What you're training is exactly what the Spain midfielder does on instinct: when your line gets cut, don't stand there wanting the ball — take the small step that makes a new angle. Keep the triangle alive. Run it for five minutes and you'll hear the change: the passing gets quicker because nobody's forcing it through a defender any more. There's always another way out.

It's the same lesson as [patient build-up](/blog/patience-is-an-attacking-skill), one layer down. Patience keeps the ball moving until the defence breaks; angles are what *let* it keep moving in the first place. No angles, no patience — just a turnover with extra steps.

## What this teaches

"Get open" turns out not to be about effort at all. The kid waving both arms, jumping up and down behind her defender, is working hard and achieving nothing. Openness isn't energy. It's a line on the floor — and a player who learns to see that line stops needing to be told to move, because she can see for herself when the ball can't reach her.

That's what Spain spent a whole tournament proving, at a level nobody could touch: the team that works hardest to give the ball somewhere to go is the team that never has to give it away. The angles come first. Everything the stars did downstream was paid for by three quiet steps sideways.
